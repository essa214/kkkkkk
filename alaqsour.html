Karnak Temple

The Karnak Temple includes ten edifices, starting with the first edifice, which is the main entrance to the temple, then a courtyard that includes several statues of the Pharaonic goddess “Sekhmet”, which was depicted in the form of a woman with the head of a lioness, which is also an image of the goddess Mut, down to another courtyard of large columns, then the Hall of the Legends Followed by the Holy of Holies Karnak also includes an obelisk made of pure granite adorned with a metal pyramid, on which some ancient texts were inscribed telling about the greatness of the sun god Ra.

Luxor temple

The ancient Egyptians called the Luxor Temple the name "Abet Reset", meaning "the southern sanctuary", because of its location within ancient Thebes (now Luxor). The temple is located about three kilometers to the south of the Karnak Temple, which was once linked by a path bounded on both sides by statues of the Sphinx and the ram-headed Sphinx. The oldest evidence of this temple dates back to the Eighteenth Dynasty (c. 1550-1295 BC).The Luxor Temple was not like most other ancient Egyptian temples on an east-west axis, but it was directed towards Karnak. Their temples at Karnak in solemn procession to the Luxor Temple so that they can visit the residing deity there, Amun im-Opet, a celebration known as the Feast of the Opet.

The Luxor Temple was not built by one king. The oldest building in it was a cabin dating back to the reign of Queen Hatshepsut (about 1473-1458 BC), while the heart of the temple was built during the reign of Amenhotep III (about 1390-1353 BC). One of the inner chambers contains a group of scenes known as scenes of divine birth, as it tells us the story of the king's lineage to the god Amun-Ra himself. The heart of the temple is preceded by a colonnaded hall, in front of which is a courtyard surrounded by columns. Amenhotep III also made the corridor of the cylinders, which consists of two rows, each row containing seven cylinders. It was decorated with scenes of the Opet Festival, which were completed during the reign of King Tutankhamun (about 1336-1327 BC) and Horemheb (about 1323-1295 BC).

Deir el-Bahari Temple

The site of Deir el-Bahari was famous for containing the mortuary temple of Queen Hatshepsut (about 1473-1458 BC) on the western mainland of Luxor. The temple consists of three graded layers in the valley, where the funeral rites of Queen Hatshepsut, in addition to her father, King Thutmose I, were held, as they were offered in offerings in order to guarantee the queen’s soul eternal life in the afterlife.The site of Deir el-Bahari carried a measure of holiness because it was associated with the deity Hathor, who patronized the kings of Egypt, led by the deity Horus, the first and legendary ruler of Egypt. It was believed that the embodiment of that deity was in the same hills whose shadow falls on the Temple of Hatshepsut, and on the other side of it where the tombs of some of the most famous kings of ancient Egypt in the modern state are located in the Valley of the Kings. We see the paintings that bear the prayers of Hathor in the form of a cow emerging from these mountains, whose top appears in the form of a pyramid.

Another king, Hatshepsut, preceded the establishment of his mortuary temple in that important area by about 600 years, and he is King Mentuhotep Nebhepetre (about 2055-2004 BC) from the Middle Kingdom, where he built his temple in a stepped form, from which Queen Hatshepsut borrowed the design of her temple.

Valley of the Kings

This site for the burial of kings was carefully chosen; Where it is located on the western bank of the Nile, because the sun god descends (dies) on the western horizon in order to be reborn, and his youth is renewed on the eastern horizon, and for this reason the West was associated with funerary concepts and most of the ancient Egyptian tombs were generally located on the western bank of the Nile for this the reason. The powerful kings of the New Kingdom are buried under the top of a pyramidal cliff that surrounds the valley. The choice of the valley to carve the royal tombs was not by chance, as the pyramid was a symbol of resurrection and eternal life, and the pyramidal shape was considered a sign of deities. This region and the summit itself were under the control of the goddess Hathor: "the Lady of the West."

Colossi of Memnon

The Colossi of Memnon is considered one of the most famous monuments of Luxor. It is located on the road leading to the tombs of the Valley of the Kings. They are what remains of an ancient temple built to perpetuate the memory of King Amenhotep III, one of the kings of the 18th Dynasty, which is considered one of the most powerful families that ruled ancient Egypt. The two statues of Memnon are considered one of the most prominent tourist attractions in Luxor, which tourists are keen to stand at, and contemplate the greatness of ancient Egyptian history. as a result of a crack in one of the statues; The air was passing through those cracks, making a sound like groaning. It was said that it was the groaning of the mother of the hero “Memnon” who was killed by “Achilles” during the Trojan Wars.

Luxor Museum

You cannot travel to Luxor without passing through the Luxor Museum, which contains more than 376 rare artifacts and mummies of the most important kings of Pharaonic Egypt. The museum is located on the Corniche Road, and it is one of the most important tourist places in Egypt to learn about the Pharaonic history. The Luxor Museum consists of two floors: the first floor contains many very important artifacts, most notably the granite head of the statue of Amenhotep III, the head of the goddess Hathor that takes the form of a cow, the statue of the god Amun, and the Karnak panel, while the upper floor contains a hall with many important statues A hall for mummies, a hall for cutting jewelry, utensils, some furniture, and amulets.

Habu City

Medinet Habu is one of the most beautiful archaeological sites in Luxor, which was built by King Ramesses III. To establish funeral rites, and to worship the god Amun. The secret of Medinet Habu's distinction in Luxor is its unique architectural style, similar to the architectural style of the temples in ancient Syria, which the king saw during his wars there. Medinet Habu was established on an area of ​​10 acres, and its walls and ceilings still retain their colors despite the date of its establishment, which dates back to 3200 BC. There are the most important tourist attractions in Luxor, most notably the Temple of Habu, one of the largest fortified temples of the ancient state, where there are two walls, one internal and the other external, a hall for ceremonies, and the place designated for priests, called the Holy of Holies.

Mummification Museum

The ancient Egyptian believed in the importance of preserving the body after death to ensure immortality in the afterlife, so mummification was a key component of the funerary practices of the ancient Egyptians, symbolically overseen by the deity Anubis. The mummification process includes many complex steps that used to take about 70 days, and is accompanied by many rituals and reading of incantations, where the organs of the deceased are carefully removed through a small incision in his body and preserved in vessels known as canopic vessels, then the body is dried using natron salt, and finally He is wrapped in rolls of linen, and magical amulets are placed inside the wraps around different parts of the mummy to protect the body. Finally, the family of the deceased carries his mummy to place it in a coffin for burial.

Valley of the Queens

The Valley of the Queens is located in the far south of the western mainland. It was built in the same architectural style as the Valley of the Kings, and for the same goal, which is the burial of women of royal families, princes, princesses, and those close to them from members of the noble class. It is located close to the Valley of the Kings. The most famous tombs in the Valley of the Queens are the tombs of Queen Nefertari, the beloved wife of Ramesses II, the most beautiful of beauties, for whom her husband made the most beautiful tomb in honor of her. All the walls of the tomb are decorated with carvings, and murals of incomparable beauty, representing the queen, receiving guidance and direction from the gods.

Banana Island

If you are tired of the historical places in Luxor, and you want to go to a different and interesting place in the midst of nature and relaxing greenery, head immediately to Banana Island, west of Luxor, which is considered a destination for tourists to enjoy the most beautiful landscapes on the Nile, and take picturesque souvenir photos. Arriving at Banana Island in Luxor It is carried out by Nile boat trips, where tourists are invited to visit it. To break the routine of archaeological sites, and enjoy a different natural atmosphere that calls for rest and relaxation. Banana Island extends over an area of ​​5 acres, and is teeming with banana trees, palm trees, and many fruit trees, and there is an old “wheel” to complete the distinctive agricultural condition of the island.

Luxor market

Visiting the Luxor markets is one of the most important tourist activities in the city, especially the Luxor tourist market, which visitors are keen to frequent, as one of the most important tourism activities in Luxor. The market is a copy similar to the Khan al-Khalili neighborhood in Cairo, where it is full of tourist attractions, antique shops, antiques, crafts, handicrafts, papyri, and accessories of a pharaonic nature, in addition to perfumery shops and clothing stores of the ancient pharaonic style. Recently, Luxor governorate carried out development and renovation operations for the market. By establishing a main gate designed in the Pharaonic style; To receive tourists in the upcoming winter tourist season.

Flying balloon

Luxor is the only governorate in Egypt that organizes flying balloon flights, competing with many European countries. It also ranks fourth in the world in balloon flying skill. Riders of the balloon hovering in the sky of Luxor see the city's natural features, such as the course of the Nile River, and the ancient temples located on the banks of the river; As this trip starts from the western mainland, which is full of hundreds of pharaonic tombs, at an altitude of 2000 feet, and in a time that ranges from 45 to 60 minutes.
