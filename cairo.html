Mosque of Muhammad Ali

The Mosque of Muhammad Ali is located inside the Citadel of Salah al-Din in Cairo. Muhammad Ali ordered its construction in the year 1265 AH / 1848 AD, in a place that was occupied by palaces dating back to the Mamluk era. This mosque was known as (the alabaster mosque) in reference to the marble slabs with which its internal and external walls were covered. The two minarets of the mosque are the highest in Egypt, with a height of 84 meters each. The mosque was built in the Turkish style. Which consists of an open courtyard and the prayer house, which consists of a square area covered by a huge central dome surrounded by four half-domes in addition to four small domes at the corners. The mosque has two pulpits, one of which is made of wood painted in green and gold, and it is the original pulpit of the mosque, while the other is made of marble and was added to the mosque later.

The tomb of Muhammad Ali Pasha is located to the right of the interior in the southwestern corner of the mosque, which was built of white marble. In the courtyard of the mosque there is a tower made of copper. Inside it is a clock that was gifted by Louis Philippe, King of France to Muhammad Ali Pasha (1262 AH / 1845 AD). Muhammad Ali Pasha responded to this gesture by gifting him the obelisk of Ramses II, which was standing in front of the Luxor Temple and today stands tall in Concorde Square in Paris

Ahmed Ibn Tulun Mosque

It was built by Ahmed Ibn Tulun (254-270 AH / 868-884 AD), the Abbasid ruler of Egypt, who became independent by its rule in the year 266 AH / 872 AD. Construction began in 263 AH / 876 AD, and its construction ended in 265 AH / 879 AD. It is located on the top of Jabal Yashkar (Ahmed Ibn Tulun Square - Al-Sayyidah Zaynab today). The main mosque of the city of Al-Qata’i is considered the new capital that Ahmed Ibn Tulun took as the seat of government, which is another step to confirm the independence of Ahmed Ibn Tulun in Egypt from the Abbasid Caliphate.

The most important characteristic of the mosque is its minaret, known as the twisted minaret, which reflects the style of the Al-Abbas Mosque in Samarra (Iraq). The arches and windows overlooking the mosque's courtyard also contain geometric and plant stucco decorations. The fountain (ablution) for ablution is located in the middle of the mosque's courtyard and is topped by a dome resting on marble columns. Inside the mosque, there are six mihrabs, "a place in the wall of the mosque where the direction of the qiblah is, and it is directed to the honorable Kaaba in Mecca, the destination of Muslims during prayer." The main mihrab of the mosque is distinguished by being hollow with very splendid decorations.

Moez Street

The history of Al-Moez Street dates back to the era of the Fatimid Caliph Al-Muizz Li Din Allah (341-365 AH / 953-975 AD), who ruled Egypt in the period between (358-365 AH / 969-975 AD) as the first Fatimid caliph of Egypt. The street is currently the largest open museum of Islamic antiquities in the world, and a unique heritage site that was included in the list of World Heritage Sites in 1979 AD. The names of the street changed throughout the historical ages, until it was known as Al-Moez Street since 1937 AD, in honor of him as the founder of Cairo. The street extends between two gates of the walls of Old Cairo, from Bab al-Futuh in the north to Bab Zuweila in the south, passing through several lanes and ancient historical streets, the most famous of which are Amir al-Jiyoush Street, al-Darb al-Asfar, Burjuan Quarter, Khan al-Khalili, and al-Ghuriya

The street includes a group of the most wonderful monuments of the city of Cairo, numbering twenty-nine monuments, which reflect a complete impression of Islamic Egypt in the period from the tenth century until the nineteenth century AD, which starts from the Fatimid era in Egypt (358-567 AH / 969-1171 AD) until the era of the Muhammad family Ali (1220-1372 AH / 1805-1953 AD). The existing antiquities vary between religious, residential, commercial and defense buildings, and today markets and traditional handicraft shops are lined along the street, which adds to the charm of the historic street.

Cairo Tower

The Cairo Tower is considered one of the prominent landmarks in Cairo, as there are many tourists who want to visit it, in order to enjoy looking at its distinguished design, which symbolizes the ancient Egyptian civilization. their temples and tombs, and at the present time the number of people flocking to this tower is increasing, especially visitors from Arab countries, who wish to climb to its roof, which is characterized by its wonderful view of Cairo, as the time to climb to the roof does not take more than seconds.

Through which the tourist can see many important places in Egypt, such as the television building, the Sphinx, the Nile, Salah al-Din Citadel, Al-Azhar, and the pyramids, in addition to the ability of visitors to eat lunch in the restaurants available in this tower, the most important of which is the circular restaurant located in The 14th floor, which is distinguished by its rotation around a fulcrum that enables the visitor to see Cairo, and contains nineteen tables, each of which can accommodate five people, and the possibility of enjoying different types of juices and drinks from the top of the tower.

Pharaonic Village

The village is a natural island in the Nile, 5 kilometers from the center of Cairo, and people dressed like ancient Egyptians do work such as agriculture, industry, religious rituals of the pharaohs, and their daily life affairs, using replica models of ancient tools. The village also includes several museums and a replica model From the tomb of Tutankhamun. The visitor begins his journey with a boat that sails through the Nile in what is called the legendary channel. To the left of the boat there is a statue of the Roman god Nilus with a group of children as a symbol of the annual flood of the Nile waters and its many bounties. After passing through the papyrus trees and a number of statues of the gods, the boat passes the visitors amid vivid scenes of the civilizations that passed through the land of Egypt.

The village includes a large group of museums such as the Museum of Pharaonic Boats, the Museum of Mummification and Ancient Egyptian Medicine, and the Museum of Arts and Beliefs. Salah al-den al-ayobi's castle

Salah El-Din Citadel is one of the most important landmarks of Islamic Cairo, and one of the oldest military castles built in the Middle Ages. Its strategic location on top of the Mokattam Mountain provided a wonderful view of all the landmarks of historical Cairo, and the impregnable walls around the capitals of Islamic Egypt with the castle provided more protection against any attack. . Salah al-Din al-Ayyubi began constructing this castle on the Mokattam Mountain in the year 572 AH / 1176 AD in a place known as the Dome of the Air, but he did not complete it during his life, and Sultan Al-Kamil bin Al-Adil (604 AH / 1207 AD) completed it. To rule Egypt until the era of Khedive Ismail, who moved the seat of government to Abdeen Palace in Khedive Cairo.

The castle witnessed many historical events since the Ayyubid era until the end of the Muhammad Ali family, which took over the rule of Egypt from the nineteenth century until the July Revolution of 1952 AD. Many facilities have been added to the castle over the ages, the most important of which are: Muhammad Ali Mosque, Al-Nasir Muhammad Ibn Qalawun Mosque, Suleiman Pasha Al-Khadim Mosque, in addition to a number of museums, including: Al-Jawhara Palace Museum, Al-Shorta, and Al-Harbi.

Egyptian Museum

The Egyptian Museum is the oldest archaeological museum in the Middle East, and houses the largest collection of ancient Egyptian antiquities in the world. The museum displays a large collection that extends from the pre-dynastic period to the Greek and Roman eras.

Among the museum's unparalleled collections are the funerary collection of Yuya and Tuya, Psusennes I and the treasures of Tanis, and the Narmer Palette commemorating the unification of Upper and Lower Egypt under a single king are among the priceless artifacts in the museum. The museum also includes wonderful statues of the great kings, Khufu, Khafre, and Menkaure, the builders of the pyramids on the Giza plateau. In addition to a large collection of papyri, coffins and ornaments that complete the distinguished collection of this museum.

The Hanging Church

This church was known as the Hanging Church because it was built over the Roman fort in ancient Egypt (Babylon Fort), and it is located next to the Coptic Museum. The church contains a nave divided into four wings separated from each other by three rows of marble columns. This nave is covered by semi-circular vaults made of wood. The church includes a baptistery of granite with inscriptions, and the noblest marble resting on ten columns with inscriptions decorated with mosaics dating back to the eleventh century AD. The eastern side also includes five structures covered with wooden domes, and the wooden ceiling of the church was built in the shape of Noah's Ark. This church is technically famous for the icons distributed on its walls, the oldest of which dates back to the fifteenth century AD, and most of them date back to the end of the eighteenth century AD.

Giza Pyramids

The Egyptian pyramids are one of the Seven Wonders of the World. It was built by the ancient Egyptians between 2630 and 1530 BC. It was also used as a tomb for kings and queens. The pyramid of Khufu, located west of Giza, is the largest among them. It is an unparalleled tourist attraction. Despite the passage of thousands of years since their construction, the pyramids are still the most powerful symbols of Egyptian civilization to this day. The pyramids are not only the goal of every visitor to Egypt, they are also a source of inspiration for art and cultural life in the country of the Nile. The pyramids were built from huge stones that were transported from the Aswan area, south of Cairo, across the Nile River, which at that time reached the pyramids area. The Greek historian states that the construction and preparation of the single pyramid took decades.

You can spend a whole day visiting the three pyramids of Egypt, where the pyramids are distinguished by their magnitude and international fame, as you can see the greatness of building these pyramids thousands of years ago, and you can take the most beautiful memorial photos there. • You can also enjoy a trip on camel back, as you can rent a camel for a certain period and take a tour around one of the three pyramids of Egypt. The Sphinx

Colossal statues are one of the hallmarks of ancient civilization, the Great Sphinx of Giza. The statue was carved in the rock of the same area in the era of the Fourth Dynasty (about 2613-2494 BC), which makes it the oldest. The ancient Egyptian Sphinxes represented the king with the body of a lion as a clear sign of his power.

Evidence indicates that the Great Sphinx was carved during the reign of King Khafra (circa 2558 - 2532 BC), the owner of the second pyramid of Giza. The Great Sphinx and its temple in front of it are located directly next to the Temple of Khafre Valley and the lower part of the ascending road leading to its mortuary temple and the pyramid. Careful archaeological analysis revealed that the Valley Temple was finished before work began on the Great Sphinx and its temple, while analysis of the Sphinx's facial features revealed striking similarities with the statues of King Khafra.

The Great Sphinx captured the imagination of travelers and explorers for thousands of years, even in ancient Egyptian times. During the Eighteenth Dynasty (about 1550-1295 BC), it became seen as a manifestation of the sun god, and it was called Hor-im-akhet, "Horus." His son, Thutmose IV (circa 1400-1390 BC), built a huge plate between his front feet, which called It bears the name of the dream board, in which he recorded an amazing event.

Pyramid of King Djoser

The Step Pyramid of King Djoser is considered one of the most famous monuments in Egypt, and it constitutes an important historical turning point in the ancient Egyptian funerary monuments. It is also considered a revolution in stone architecture and royal burial. In addition to its huge size, it is the first pyramid built by the ancient Egyptians and the oldest known stone building. The age of building the Step Pyramid dates back to the beginning of the Third Dynasty, during the reign of King Nercht (about 2667-2648 BC), now known as Djoser, which is the most common.

Before his reign, Egyptian kings and elites were buried on mastabas. The term "mastaba" refers to a type of funerary building that was generally rectangular in shape and built over a burial well, which was underground. The step pyramid consists of six terraces that give the stepped shape, and the engineer Imhotep was the author of this great innovation.

At one end of the pyramidal group is located a building known as the Southern Cemetery, which is considered a symbolic tomb for King Djoser, perhaps reflecting his role as king of Upper and Lower Egypt. And the renewal of his strength, and the stone booths on the eastern side of the courtyard were used in this ceremony, ensuring that the king would continue to renew his youth forever.
